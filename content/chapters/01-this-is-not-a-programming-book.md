# This is not a programming book

There are a lot of books about programming. Many of them are called a variant of [_Programming for Dummies_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/For_Dummies). This is not one of those.

Most programming books teach you how to behave inside the systems other people built. This one is more interested in how those systems got there, why they are shaped so strangely, and what you are allowed to do once you stop treating them as laws of nature.

That is what I mean by _wizard_ here. Not a rank, not a credential, and not a secret club. A wizard is someone willing to look at a problem long enough to wonder whether the problem was shaped badly in the first place.

You may already have been programming computers for a while. You may not be happy about the state of software and programming in general. Or you may simply have noticed that the tools around you are human inventions, which means they can be questioned, repaired, replaced, and made kinder.

To understand what you can change, we will need to go backwards. Again and again, this book will dig up the moment where some familiar piece of programming was still unfixed, undecided, and strange.

That is partly history, but mostly permission. Everything you have learned was once something someone thought up. There is no law of nature that forces you to keep thinking inside someone else’s invention. You are allowed to question the shapes you inherited, and sometimes to make a better one.

That is the point of this book.

Not that you should reject everything. Wizards don't waste good magic. They steal, improve, combine, and sometimes very carefully throw things away. The trick is to notice which part of the world is actually fixed, and which part is only a habit that got old enough to look like nature.

## The wizard's question

A beginner asks: _How do I solve this?_

A better programmer asks: _What is the simplest solution?_

A wizard asks something stranger:

> What would make this problem disappear?

This is not laziness. Or at least not only laziness. It is the ability to look at the shape of a problem and suspect that the shape itself may be wrong.

Most of the chapters in this book are stories about people doing exactly that. They changed the notation. They changed the machine. They changed the boundary between systems. They changed the language people used to talk about the work.

And after that, everyone else said the new thing was obvious.

That is how magic usually works.

## A ruin worth exploring

This book is going to return to the Web more than once.

That is not because the Web is the only interesting place to learn programming. It is because the Web is one of the few places where the whole strange machinery is still visible. You can find language there, and documents, and addresses, and protocols, and old compromises that somehow became permanent architecture. You can find beautiful ideas buried under twenty years of tooling. You can find tiny inventions that changed the world, and enormous systems built to avoid understanding them.

The Web is also one of the few great software systems that still lets you lift the carpet. View source is not what it used to be, but the old spell has not completely vanished. A page can still be text. An address can still be typed. A link can still point from one mind to another without asking permission from a platform.

So yes, this book will be web-heavy. Not because every wizard must build websites, but because the Web is a very good ruin to explore. It is old enough to contain fossils, alive enough to contain monsters, and simple enough in places that you can still see the original bones.

We will use it the way wizards use ruins: not to admire the past, not to restore it exactly, but to steal working magic from it.
